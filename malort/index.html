Jeppson's Malrt:
A search for the origins of Chicago's liquor

Reported by Meg Anderson

Produced by Jin Wu

Chicago is known for its beloved blues, improv comedy, architecture, and deep-dish pizza, to name a few. But there's another Chicago staple that often gets overlooked until you make the move to the Windy City. It's called Jeppson's Malört and it's got a notorious reputation as a beverage with a kick.

Many Chicagoans consider Malört, an alcohol made from the Wormwood herb, a rite of passage for living in their city. The spirit is known for an intensely bitter taste, but despite its notoriously nasty flavor, Malört inspires endless devotion from its proud fans. It has made appearances in major motion pictures, inspired comedians and is the subject of a new documentary.

Jeppson's Malört is hardly a new craze. It's been around for almost a century, but in the last 10 years, sales have soared. Why all the fuss? Reporter Meg Anderson went on a search across the city to find out.

Jeppson's Malört turns its drinkers into poets.

JOHN GREEN: It tastes like a punch in the face. It tastes like pencil shavings. Rotten grapefruit. Those are the ones that stand out: the pencil shavings, the punch in the face particularly.

That was bartender John Green at Simon's bar talking about Malört, an alcohol made from the bitter Wormwood herb. Here's bartender Adam Dreyer at The Bad Apple.

ADAM DREYER: You go to Mexico and have your first shot of tequila, you go to Chicago and have your first shot of Malört, and then you make a face and then everyone says "ooh," and then you're mad for a little bit, but then you start thinking of who you can do that to as well.

I did think about it. So, I brought a bottle over to a dinner party at a friend's house.

Despite being notoriously nasty, Malört is considered a Chicago rite of passage. Its production moved to Florida in the '80s, but before that, it was made in Chicago for most of the twentieth century and is still sold almost entirely in the city. A local band, Archie Powell and the Exports, even wrote a song about it.

Nat Pop: The Malört Song: A Waltz for Old Jeppson

So, why is Jeppson's Malört such a Chicago thing? I went on a search to find out. My first stop was back at Simon's — it's a Swedish bar that officially opened the year after prohibition was repealed. Owner Scott Martin showed me around.

SCOTT MARTIN: This operated from 1929 to 1933, so as we go down these stairs, just walk sideways, just like I'm walking and hold onto the rail and face this way. It's a narrow stairway. So, it literally stood for the No Name club, so you didn't mention who was going here. You didn't mention nothing, right, that's what they mean by speakeasy.

Surviving prohibition is something Simon's and Malört have in common. Carl Jeppson emigrated from Sweden near the turn of the century and brought over a recipe for a beskbrännvin — a bitter, wormwood liquor that was popular in Sweden at the time. Malört actually means wormwood in Swedish. During prohibition, Jeppson sold Malört as a medicine to soothe indigestion. In the mid–1930s, he sold his recipe to a man named George Brode.

Down in the speakeasy, Martin played old–timey music on a phonograph to set the scene.

Nat Pop: Old Music

He had a shelf full of Malört bottles down there. From 1934 onward, Simon's made a name for itself as a Malört bar, drawing the attention of Brode.

MARTIN: I once had George Brode call up the bar one day and I got on the phone and he said, "Who is this?" and I said, "This is Scott. Who is this?" and he said, "This is George Brode. I own Malört." And I said, "Oh! Wow! Hey, what's up? That's cool. What are you calling for?" And he said, "What are you guys doing there?" And I went, "What do you mean?" And he's like, "How are you going through two cases of Malört a month?" And I said, "Everybody likes it up here!"

Brode pushed Malört's limits beyond Chicago's Swedish population and spread it across the city. When he bought Malört, he owned a distillery, but when he sold that company, Malört was the one brand he decided to keep for himself. It became his hobby and he wasn't afraid to be playful with the brand's marketing.

MARTIN: You know, the original literature they had on there, fifteen, eighteen years ago: Are you man enough to drink our two–fisted liquor? Our liquor is rugged and unrelenting, even brutal to the palate. Peculiar way of advertising but it worked.

Malört has been experiencing a sort of golden age lately. Its sales have climbed by over 80 percent in recent years, and as the craft cocktail scene has grown, Chicago bartenders consider it a challenge to make a delicious Malört drink. Bartender Mike Melazzo at Mott St. showed me how.

MELAZZO: You know, everyone is familiar with Malört because it's like that joke shot you would do with your friends "oh its so bitter, it's too bitter," but you know, you use it in the right context and it can actually make a cocktail that's too sweet, it can help balance it out really nicely.

Nat Pop: Cocktail making noises

MELAZZO: And now we have a twist on a CEO — probably because of the Malört, we should probably call it an Enron or something.

Melazzo didn't actually use Jeppson's Malört in that cocktail — he used Letherbee's Besk — a Malört–inspired liquor developed by Analogue owner Robby Haynes.

HAYNES: One night, yeah, I was out at a bar and I thought, oh this will be great, make a boutique version of this really bitter and often kind of misaligned Swedish liquor.

A 2013 lawsuit banned Letherbee's from using the name Malört, but Haynes thinks the two are different enough that there is room for both.

HAYNES: It's a yin yang, for sure. They both, kind of, I see them both sitting in different places. Jeppson's has their thing. They have an identity. They are an institution, even.

It was time for me to go find that institution.

GABELICK: I'm Pat Gabelick and I'm the proprietor of Jeppson's Malört.

MECHLING: I'm Sam Mechling and I'm the director of marketing for Jeppson's Malört.

Gabelick and Mechling are the only two managing the company. George Brode hired Gabelick as a secretary in 1966. He left her the company when he died in 1999, but she hadn't even tried the liquor until recently.

GABELICK: We always had a bottle in the office. I don't think I ever drank it. I smelled the dry wormwood. I never took a shot of Malört. I probably had my first shot, well, after he died.

Mechling, on the other hand, was hooked. He started designing and selling Malört T–Shirts at the bar where he works. He met Gabelick in 2012 when she came in with a lawyer to stop him from violating Malört's copyright. She liked what she saw, and he's been in charge of Malört's marketing ever since.

MECHLING: That was sort of the start of it. I mean, it was, I think she thought I was a con man trying to steal the brand.

GABELICK: Well, I didn't know.

MECHLING: You didn't know.

GABELICK: I had no idea.

MECHLING: And I thought you were this evil liquor baroness trying to ruin my life, so it was very pleasant to find out none of those things were true.

Mechling has a theory for why Malört has stood the test of time.

MECHLING: It's a camaraderie thing. There's this expression that says there are no strangers in foxholes, because if you survive something traumatic, it brings you closer, and I think the thing that's most interesting is the generation before us, it was all about "This isn't your dad's whiskey," because it was, like, kind of lame to be like your parents, but now, kids want to be like their parents. They wear the same types of clothes. They listen to vinyl records. So, it's cool to harken back to things that came before us and Malört is very much cemented as an old Chicago thing.

Gabelick says she likes hearing people's Malört stories but wishes her old boss George Brode were here to see how it has taken off.

GABELICK: I don't know if he would believe it, because of the volume. We never sold the kind of volume that we do now. He would just be in seventh heaven.

MECHLING: And I think he would be proud to see that we got there not by underselling the flavor. We got there by touting the flavor, because for him that's what made us special and that's what made us unique is that we cater to a certain type of person, and that's who has taken us where we are.

In a sense, Malört is a little like Chicago itself. It can be tough and brutal and not for the faint of heart. But if you stick it out, maybe you'll be glad you did.

For Medill, I'm Meg Anderson.

Early 1900s

The United States bans the sale, production, import and transport of alcoholic beverages. Carl Jeppson begins selling Jeppson'ss Malört as a medicine to soothe indigestion.

1920-1933

With the end of prohibition, Carl Jeppson established Jeppson'ss Malört as a non-medicinal spirit.

1934

Malört becomes a staple in the Swedish community on the north side. Lawyer George Brode, the owner of Bielzoff Products, a local distillery, buys the company and begins to expand the drink to Polish and other immigrant communities across the city.

Mid 1930s

George Brode begins practicing law full time and sells all of Bielzoff except for one product: He keeps Malört as a pet project and forms the Carl Jeppson Company.
